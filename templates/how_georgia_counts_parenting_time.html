How Georgia Counts Parenting Time Days

The complete guide to understanding Georgia's overnight rule and parenting time calculations for child support Schedule C

The Short Answer: Georgia counts parenting time days using the overnight rule. The parent with whom the child sleeps overnight receives credit for one parenting day. Courts average parenting time over a two-year period and enter the yearly average on Child Support Schedule C.

The Overnight Rule Explained

Georgia courts use one simple principle to count parenting time: where does the child sleep? The parent who has the child overnight gets credit for that day.

This approach provides clarity and consistency across all Georgia custody cases. Rather than debating hours or activities, courts focus on a single measurable factor: overnight stays.

Key Principle: If your child goes to sleep at your home tonight, you receive credit for 1.0 parenting day regardless of what time the child arrived or when the child will leave tomorrow.

Why the Overnight Rule?

The overnight rule exists because providing shelter overnight is a significant parenting expense. Parents who have children overnight incur costs for housing, utilities, bedding, food for breakfast and dinner, and maintaining a suitable living space.

Georgia's child support formula recognizes that both parents contribute to the child's expenses. The parenting time adjustment reduces the noncustodial parent's support obligation to account for the direct costs they bear during their parenting time.

How to Count Days Step-by-Step

Standard Overnight Counting

Each night the child sleeps at your residence counts as one full parenting day (1.0). This applies whether the child arrives at 3:00 PM or 8:00 PM the day before.

Scenario Who Gets Credit Days Credited
Child sleeps at Father's home Friday night Father 1.0
Child picked up at 5 PM, returned at 9 AM Picking-up parent (overnight) 1.0
Two-week summer vacation Vacation parent 14.0
Exchange happens at noon on Sunday Parent who had Saturday overnight 1.0 (for Saturday)

Partial Days (0.5 Credit)

Some custody arrangements include substantial daytime visits without overnights. These may be counted as half-days (0.5) when the visit is significant, typically four or more hours.

Important: Partial day credits are not automatic. Courts evaluate whether daytime-only visits warrant 0.5 credit based on the duration and nature of the parenting time. Standard visitation without overnights generally does not receive credit.

Why Georgia Uses a Two-Year Average

Georgia law requires courts to average parenting time over a two-year (24-month) period rather than a single year. This requirement exists because custody schedules vary between years.

Factors That Vary Year to Year

  • Alternating Holidays: Many parenting plans alternate holidays by even/odd years. Thanksgiving might be with Mother in even years and Father in odd years.
  • Leap Years: Leap years have 366 days instead of 365, affecting percentage calculations.
  • School Calendar Variations: Spring break dates, teacher workdays, and semester schedules shift annually.
  • Weekend Patterns: The number of Fridays, fifth weekends, and holiday weekends varies by year.
Yearly Average = Total Days Over 24 Months ÷ 2
Example: If Parent B has 240 days over a two-year period, the yearly average is 120 days (240 ÷ 2).

The two-year average provides a more accurate picture of the actual custody arrangement and prevents unfair calculations based on anomalous single-year data.

Schedule C and the Parenting Time Adjustment

Child Support Schedule C is the official Georgia worksheet where parenting time calculations are recorded. The yearly average from your two-year calculation goes directly on this form.

OCGA 19-6-15(b)(5.1) requires courts to "adjust the noncustodial parent's share of the basic child support obligation amount to account for such parent's expenses incurred during his or her court ordered parenting time."

How Schedule C Works

Schedule C converts parenting days into a percentage adjustment. More parenting time means a larger reduction in the noncustodial parent's child support obligation.

Annual Parenting Days Percentage of Time Impact on Support
52 days (every other weekend) 14.2% Small adjustment
100 days 27.4% Moderate adjustment
130 days 35.6% Significant adjustment
182.5 days (50/50) 50.0% Maximum adjustment

What Counts (and What Doesn't)

What IS Counted as Parenting Time

  • Overnight stays at parent's residence
  • Extended vacation periods with overnights
  • Holiday overnights as specified in parenting plan
  • School break overnights (spring break, fall break, winter break)
  • Summer vacation overnights
  • Makeup overnights ordered by court

What is NOT Counted

  • Daytime visits without overnights (generally)
  • Time child spends at school or daycare
  • Transportation time between households
  • Informal arrangements not in court order
  • Time parent spends with child at other locations (sports, activities)
  • Phone calls, video chats, or virtual visitation
Court Order Requirement: Only parenting time established by court order, consent agreement, or filed parenting plan may be counted. Informal arrangements between parents, even if longstanding, do not qualify for the parenting time adjustment.

2026 Law Changes: From Deviation to Adjustment

Effective January 1, 2026, Georgia Senate Bill 454 made significant changes to how parenting time affects child support calculations.

Before January 1, 2026

Under the old law, the "parenting time deviation" was discretionary. Judges could choose whether to grant it and determine the amount based on their assessment of the case. This led to inconsistent outcomes across different courts and judges.

After January 1, 2026

The new law replaces the discretionary deviation with a mandatory "parenting time adjustment." Courts must now:

  • Calculate the number of parenting days for each parent
  • Average the days over a two-year period
  • Enter the figures on Schedule C
  • Apply the adjustment automatically using the Schedule C formula
What This Means for You: The parenting time adjustment is no longer discretionary. If you have court-ordered parenting time, you are entitled to have it reflected in your child support calculation through Schedule C.

Frequently Asked Questions: Georgia Parenting Time Calculation

How do I calculate parenting time days in Georgia?

To calculate parenting time days in Georgia for child support purposes, count each overnight the noncustodial parent has with the child over a 12-month period. Georgia uses the overnight rule, meaning the parent with whom the child sleeps receives credit for that day. For recurring daytime periods without overnights, divide total annual hours by 24 to get fractional day credit. Courts require a two-year average to account for alternating holidays.

What is the parenting time adjustment in Georgia child support?

The parenting time adjustment in Georgia child support allows the presumptive support amount to be adjusted when the noncustodial parent has significant parenting time. Effective January 1, 2026, Georgia law requires courts to calculate this mandatory adjustment using Schedule C. The adjustment reduces the noncustodial parent's obligation based on the percentage of time the child spends with them.

How many parenting time days triggers an adjustment in Georgia?

Under OCGA § 19-6-15(b)(5.1), the parenting time adjustment may apply when parenting time exceeds the threshold specified in the statute. The adjustment percentage is calculated based on the noncustodial parent's share of annual parenting days divided by 365. Courts average parenting time over a 24-month period to determine the applicable adjustment.

Does Georgia count overnights or 24-hour periods?

Georgia counts overnights (not 24-hour periods) when calculating parenting time days for child support adjustment purposes. Under OCGA § 19-6-15, "days" means: (i) the total number of overnights a parent spends with the child, or (ii) for regular recurring daytime periods, the total hours divided by 24.

How do holidays affect parenting time calculations in Georgia?

Holidays affect Georgia parenting time calculations by adding or subtracting days from the regular schedule. Most parenting plans alternate holidays between parents in even and odd years. This is why Georgia requires courts to average parenting time over a two-year period—to capture the full effect of alternating holiday schedules.
